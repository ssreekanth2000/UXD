Final Design

Submitted: 12/10/19

Introduction

My Foodwáy is an application for front-of-house employees and managers in the restaurant/food industry. Our application allows for easy access and quick navigation of the menu to find ingredient information. This platform can then be used to answer customer questions about dietary restrictions.

How It Works

The employee starts the process by opening up the application.

They are then taken to the home screen where the full menu is displayed with icons for allergen information.

If the customer asks about allergen information for a specific menu item, the employee can use the food category navigation on the left side to narrow the food options in the full menu list.

If a customer asks for what items fit their dietary restrictions, the employee could use the toggles in the right corner to filter the list by the different types of restrictions. The list is also filtered by items that originally fit the dietary restrictions and then below are the items that can be modified to fit the dietary restriction. The bottom right corner has a key that explains why some icons are grayed out.

When the employee clicks on a menu item in the list, they are brought to a page specific to that item. This page displays the ingredients and allergen restrictions. The restaurant can also notes about the specific allergen information and modifications that can be made for that dish.

We also provide the users with a search bar for if they need to directly find a specific dish, quickly. This however is not a priority feature of our product as typing on tablets can be a hassle.

How Did We Arrive At This Design

Our problem space originally included small businesses in general.  In this space, we came up with a design to allow for quick and painless inventory and personal searching (See picture below).

With this design we had attempted to alleviate difficulties with internal communications by having a common platform through which to edit inventory and information.  This was a problem we had identified in other businesses from our interviews and analysis leading up to that point, but it was most exemplified by our Busy Brian persona and thus geared toward users in that space.

Busy Brian is a persona that owns a restaurant and has many employees and managers that work under him.  As such we expected this to be effective for Brian.  However, as we talked to users and focused more on specifically restaurant owners, we came to notice the varied and often inefficient methods different restaurants utilize to inform front-of-house workers of allergen and dietary information about the menu items.  One example is Panera, where employees had to pull out a 3 ring binder and manually search for allergen information when asked.

Through these insights, we realized that one of Brian’s major needs is for all employees to have consistent and quick communication with customers especially for information as important as allergens.  This led us to prioritize ease of navigation, flexibility, and information acquisition speed.  These criteria led us to the design shown above.

How Our Design Meets User Needs

One comment we heard multiple times from various restaurant owners is that this type of application could be the most useful for employees that are not fully familiar with the menu be more confident when speaking to customers about their dietary restrictions and not have to find a senior staff member to answer the question. This not only gives autonomy to those employees, but increases customer trust in employees as they can confidently answer and explain why something does or doesn’t fit dietary restrictions.  Because of this goal, we prioritized making the screen readable and intuitive to use - examples of this include making the icons large and appear in a consistent column, and working to make clickable text look like buttons so that it is easy to know where to press for filtering or more information.

How User Input Informed Our Design

Before our final design, we had two versions of information presentation for our system: A table layout version, and a list layout version. To determine which design direction we should go we consulted with two users. These users both liked the system but saw places for improvement. Both preferred the table format, however felt that the filtering functionality needed clearer affordances. We had also showed the switch version previously to users and had come up with the table format on the basis of feedback from those users.

We checked different metrics like response time, access time and misclicks and found out in the usability test that the table format was preferred by the users we tested with.

Final Design Changes

In the final design for Foodwáy, we changed the styling of the filtering links and menu item links to make them look more button like. By creating a more 3-dimensional style button, we give the user an affordance that signals that they can click on those links to take them to a new page.

           

The style for the new buttons are on the left while the old buttons are on the right.

We also made the size of the individual menu items in the list larger in order to have less cluttered information on the page. This also makes the information more readable for when the employee is in a rush.

Possible Future Improvements and Implementations

Given more time, there are a few things that we would like the change and implement. One functionality that we had hoped to implement was a more robust design for in stock/out of stock designations for ingredients/dishes, and other notes for the dishes that may affect the availability to the customer. Another functionality we would like to include is viable substitutions for items in a dish, so that if a dish can be changed to meet a customers dietary needs, the employee can quickly suggest an alternative.

There was also some specific feedback on how to make sure our product is viable in the business setting. Many of the users interviewed expressed concern about the data entry aspect of the platform, something we chose not to focus on for our project. However, we acknowledge that for this product to be viable, any data entry method must be easy for the business owner to complete, and must either be a process that takes very little time or a process that still works with incremental addition of data. Another piece of feedback we received for what would make this product more viable for restaurant owners would be integration of this application with the business’s Point of Sales (POS) system. Doing this would make it simpler for managers/employees to adopt this application, and make it so that a lot of their information is in one place.
